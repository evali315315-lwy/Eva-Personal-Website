On Slow Living

Personal 4 min read January 5, 2026
#lifestyle #mindfulness #productivity

In a world that constantly tells us to hustle, optimize, and maximize every moment, I've been conducting a quiet experiment: What happens when we deliberately slow down?

This isn't about laziness or lack of ambition. It's about questioning the assumption that faster is always better, that productivity is the ultimate measure of a life well-lived.

The Cult of Speed

We live in an era obsessed with speed. Fast food, fast fashion, fast responses. Our phones buzz with notifications demanding immediate attention. We're expected to reply to emails within minutes, to be "always on," constantly available.

This acceleration has costs. Burnout rates are soaring. Anxiety and depression are epidemic. We move so quickly from one task to the next that we rarely stop to ask whether we're moving in the right direction.

My Experiment with Slowness

Three months ago, I decided to try something different. I would deliberately introduce slowness into my daily routine:

  • Taking walks without my phone or any destination
  • Cooking meals from scratch instead of ordering takeout
  • Reading physical books instead of scrolling social media
  • Having conversations without checking the time
  • Working on one task at a time, with full attention

The first week was uncomfortable. My mind resisted. It wanted to multitask, to check my phone, to do more, faster. I felt an anxious pull toward productivity, toward visible output.

What I Discovered

But as the weeks passed, something shifted. I started noticing things I'd missed in my rush through life. The way morning light changes throughout the season. The subtle flavors in food when you eat slowly and attentively. The depth of conversations when you're not halfway out the door.

Slowness is not about doing less. It's about being more present with what you do.

The Paradox of Productivity

Counterintuitively, slowing down made me more effective. When I focused on one task at a time, I did better work. When I took breaks to walk and think, I had better ideas. When I slept more and stressed less, I had more energy.

The productivity culture would have us believe that we need to squeeze every drop from every moment. But humans aren't machines. We need rest, reflection, and space to process experience into wisdom.

Finding Your Own Pace

I'm not suggesting everyone should adopt my specific practices. What works for me might not work for you. The point isn't to follow a prescribed routine but to question the default assumption that faster is better.

Ask yourself: What would it feel like to have a day with nothing scheduled? To spend an hour doing something with no productive outcome? To simply sit and think, or sit and do nothing at all?

These questions might make you uncomfortable. That discomfort is worth examining.

Slowness as Resistance

In our current economic system, there's immense pressure to commodify every moment. Your attention is valuable to advertisers. Your labor is valuable to employers. Even your rest is packaged and sold back to you as "self-care."

To claim time for slowness, for activities with no monetary value or productive output, is a radical act. It's an assertion that your life is more than your productivity, that your worth isn't measured in tasks completed or items checked off a list.

The Journey Continues

I'm still learning what it means to live slowly in a fast world. Some days I slip back into old patterns, rushing from one thing to another. But I'm becoming more aware of when I'm moving too fast, more able to choose a different pace.

If you're feeling overwhelmed, burned out, or disconnected from your own life, I encourage you to experiment with slowness. Start small. Take one walk this week with no destination or device. Cook one meal with full attention. Read one chapter of a book without checking your phone.

Notice what happens. Notice what you feel. The answers might surprise you.
